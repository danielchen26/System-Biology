\newsl{Apoptosis}

programmed cell death --cellular suicide-- is called apoptosis
(from the Greek for ``a falling off'')
\medskip

apoptosis is a necessary part of the
development of many multicellular organisms
\medskip

some cells play only transient role during development; when no
longer needed, they receive signals that induce apoptosis
\medskip

(e.g. tail of a tadpole, which is not needed by the adult frog)
\medskip

compared with death caused by stress or injury, apoptosis tidy:
\medskip

rather than spill their contents into the environment, 

apoptotic cells quietly implode, thus ensuring 

no detrimental effects on the surrounding tissue

\end{frame}\newsl{Caspases}

apoptosis is invoked by caspase proteins, which are always present in the
cell, but lie dormant until activated
\medskip

the family of caspase proteins is split
into two categories:
\medskip
%\bi
%\item

$\bullet$
\emph{initiator caspases} respond to apoptosis-inducing stimuli 
\medskip

are triggered externally (via transmembrane receptors) 

\medskip
or internally, by stress
signals from the mitochondria (e.g., starvation signals)
\medskip

$\bullet$
\emph{executioner caspases} activated by initiator caspases
\medskip

they carry out the
task of cellular destruction by cleaving a number of key proteins and
activating DNases that degrade the cell's DNA

\end{frame}\newsl{Model}

\emph{Eissing et al, 2004}

caspase-8, an initiator, and caspase-3, an executioner
\medskip

Caspase-8 is triggered by external stimuli

When active, it activates caspase-3
\medskip

Caspase proteins activated by removal of a
masking domain, revealing a catalytic site 
\medskip

This cleavage is irreversible: protein only can be degraded

Also production processes for each protein

\medskip
to guarantee decision to undergo apoptosis is irreversible,

feedback mechanism: active caspase-3 activates caspase-8 
\medskip

positive feedback ensures caspase activity is self-perpetuating
\medskip

in addition to caspases, model
incorporates two proteins,

 IAP and BAR, which inhibit apoptosis by binding active caspases, forming
inert complexes

\end{frame}\newsl{Model, ctd}

\picc{0.4}{fig615_ingalls_apoptosis.png}

\emph{Eising apoptosis model (shown not as a chemical reaction network, but 
using a different graphical representation)}

\end{frame}\newsl{Players in model}

An extracellular signal triggers
  activation of 
  caspase-8 (C8*) from its inactive form (C8) 

\medskip
Once active, caspase-8
  activates caspase-3 (C3 to C3*) 

\medskip
Active caspase-3 activates caspase-8,
  forming a positive feedback loop 

\medskip
Two apoptotic inhibitors are included: BAR and IAP 
\medskip

These proteins
  bind active caspase-8 and -3, respectively, thus inhibiting the progression
  to apoptosis 
\medskip

(Dots indicate degraded proteins)

do not consume the enzyme.  The authors implicitly assume these

\end{frame}\newsl{Behavior of model}

at low levels of activated caspase, at rest in a ``life'' state
\medskip

when caspase activity rises above a threshold, 
\medskip

positive feedback commits the system to reaching steady state 
\medskip

with high levels of active caspase, a ``death'' state
(of course, this state is transient - the cell is being dismantled -- only
``quasi'' steady state, on the timescale of signaling)

\medskip

simulation shows the response of the system to an input signal:

begins at rest with zero input and low caspase activity
\medskip

input $=>$ slow increase in the activity level of caspase-8
\medskip

eventually leads to a
rapid rise in caspase activity,
\medskip

and system settles into ``death'' state with high caspase activity
\medskip

\mbox{input signal removed: this self-perpetuating state
persists (bistablility)}

since input signal does not cause a return to the initial state, 
\medskip

transition is irreversible

\end{frame}\newsl{Plots}

\picc{0.4}{fig616_ingalls_apoptosis.png}

\end{frame}\newsl{Plots}

simulation begins in the low caspase activity ``life'' state
\medskip

at time t =100 minutes, an input signal (Input=200) is introduced, causing an
increase in caspase-8 activity
\medskip

this triggers a positive feedback loop that results in a rapid
increase in activated caspase-8 and caspase-3 abundance at about t = 300
\medskip

system then settles to the high caspase-activity ``death'' state 
\medskip

input
stimulus is removed at time t =1200 minutes, but there is no effect: the
apoptotic switch is irreversible.

\end{frame}
